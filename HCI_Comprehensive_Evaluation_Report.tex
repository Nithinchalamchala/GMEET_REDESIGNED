\section{Introduction}
This report comprehensively documents the interactive redesign of Google Meet. In strict adherence to the End-Semester assignment rubric, this high-fidelity prototype bypasses generic wireframes to offer a fully interactive HTML/CSS/JS frontend. Every visual interface choice implicitly respects a requested Psychological concept, Classical Design Law, or Usability heuristic.

\section{Shneiderman's 8 Golden Rules \& Nielsen's Guidelines}

\subsection{Error Prevention (Nielsen \#5 \& Shneiderman \#5)}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Pre-Meeting Network Check}: Users verify mic, camera, and connection stability before they can even click join.
    \item \textbf{Destructive Action Confirmation}: Leaving the meeting triggers a secondary confirmation modal.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Visibility of System Status (Nielsen \#1) \& Feedback (Shneiderman \#3)}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Muted Status}: Pressing \texttt{Space} to mute triggers an assertive multi-modal visual (a red animated banner and border).
\end{itemize}
\imageplaceholder{Mute Feedback Banner}{Inside index.html, click Mute. Screenshot the red `You are muted` banner popping up.}{Provides immediate, unambiguous visual system status Feedback.}

\subsection{Vital Few / 80-20 Rule}
The primary bottom control bar was strictly reduced to 7 buttons that constitute 80\% of user interactions.

If a user clicks the "Mute" button five times manually, the system organically surfaces a tooltip indicating they can use the \texttt{Spacebar}.

\section{Closure \& External Memory}

\subsection{Meeting Summary Interface}
The concept of \textbf{Closure} states that operations should have distinct beginnings, middles, and ends. When leaving a meeting, instead of abruptly dropping the user back to the dashboard, the system renders a \textbf{Meeting Summary}.
Furthermore, this serves to reduce reliance on the user's \textbf{External Memory} (Cognitive load reduction), by summarizing chat logs, auto-generated notes, and session duration immediately after the call concludes.
\imageplaceholder{Meeting Summary / Closure Screen}{Open index.html, click 'Leave', confirm. Screenshot the 'Meeting Summary' layout displayed.}{Applies Gestalt Closure and reduces reliance on short-term external memory.}

\section{Robustness \& Asimov's Laws}
System faults should never unpredictably drop user state (Asimov's Law of non-harm). 
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Network Failure Modal}: We implemented a robustness layer simulating network failure. The interface freezes with a non-destructive "Reconnecting..." overlay instead of kicking the user out.
\end{itemize}

\section{Cognitive Defense \& Persuasive Support Systems}

\subsection{Anthropomorphic User Support Systems (Meeting Assistant)}
When users struggle silently, legacy systems fail to notice. If our AI detects inactivity (no speaking), a non-obtrusive \textbf{Meeting Assistant Bubble} surfaces gracefully: \textit{"I'm observing you are not speaking... want me to lower your mic?"} This satisfies Proactive Support System Principles.
\imageplaceholder{Proactive Assistant Tooltip}{Open index.html natively and wait 15 seconds without speaking/clicking. Screenshot the avatar bubble popping up.}{Proactive, empathetic user support preventing accidental background noise.}

\subsection{Nudge Theory: Anti-Fatigue 20-20-20 Tip}
To defend against "Zoom Fatigue", the system deploys Thaler's \textbf{Nudge Theory}. During long sessions, a non-obtrusive tooltip naturally appears advocating the \textbf{20-20-20 Timebreak Rule} (Look 20 feet away, for 20 seconds, every 20 mins) to aggressively lower Cortisol and visual stress.

    \item \textbf{Recency}: The highly destructive "Leave" button is pinned precisely at the extreme end (Right).

\section{Poka-Yoke Error Prevention: Privacy Shield}
In Japanese Lean Manufacturing, \textbf{Poka-Yoke} is the concept of ``mistake-proofing'' — designing systems so that errors become \textit{physically impossible} rather than merely warned against. This is also a direct implementation of \textbf{Nielsen's \#5 (Error Prevention)} and \textbf{Asimov's First Law} (a system must not, through inaction, allow a human to come to harm).

When a user clicks ``Present Now'' to share their screen, the system does \textbf{not} immediately begin sharing. Instead, an intercept modal — the \textbf{Privacy Shield} — halts the action and displays:

\begin{quote}
\textit{``Privacy Shield Active: Detected 3 unread OS messages. Your notifications have been auto-suppressed to prevent embarrassing leaks while presenting.''}
\end{quote}

The user must explicitly acknowledge the shield and click ``Acknowledge \& Present'' to proceed. They can also abort entirely with ``Cancel Share''. This transforms a potentially catastrophic social error (accidentally exposing private messages to 50 people) into a deliberate, conscious action.

\imageplaceholder{Privacy Shield Intercept Modal}{Open index.html, click the 'Present' button in the bottom control bar. Screenshot the full-screen overlay showing the shield emoji, warning text, and the two action buttons.}{Demonstrates Poka-Yoke mistake-proofing: the system physically prevents the user from accidentally sharing private content by inserting a mandatory acknowledgement step.}

\section{Spatial Audio Canvas (Direct Physical Mental Models)}
Traditional video grids are a \textbf{Legacy Design} artifact — they map participants into rigid rows and columns that have no spatial meaning. In the real world, we modulate attention and volume by \textit{physically walking closer} to someone. Our Spatial Canvas view directly maps this real-world mental model into the digital interface.

\subsection{How It Works}
Clicking the new ``Spatial'' tab in the view toggle transforms the grid into a freeform 2D canvas. Each participant becomes a \textbf{draggable circular avatar} floating on a radial-gradient background. The user can:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Drag any participant} closer or farther from themselves.
    \item The system simulates \textbf{proximity-based volume adjustment}: closer = louder, farther = softer.
    \item Participants brought to the front visually overlap others (z-index stacking), reinforcing the physical depth illusion.
\end{itemize}

This directly satisfies the rubric requirement for \textbf{Interaction Design Paradigms \& Mental Models}: the interface replaces abstract dropdown sliders with a visceral, intuitive spatial metaphor that users already understand from real life.

Screenshot the circular participant avatars scattered across the radial-gradient canvas, then drag one closer and screenshot the proximity toast.}{Demonstrates the Physical Mental Model paradigm: Distance = Volume, replacing legacy abstractions with real-world spatial interaction.}

\section{Advanced Fitts's Law: Infinite Edge Panic Mute}

\subsection{Theoretical Foundation}
Fitts's Law models human motor targeting time as:

\[
T = a + b \cdot \log_2\left(\frac{D}{W} + 1\right)
\]

where $D$ is distance to target and $W$ is target width. In most interfaces, $W$ is finite (e.g., a 48px button). However, the \textbf{edges and corners of a physical screen} have a unique property: they have \textbf{infinite effective width} because the mouse cursor physically cannot overshoot past them. This means the Index of Difficulty drops to essentially \textbf{zero}.

\subsection{Implementation}
We placed an invisible 50×50px trigger zone in the absolute \textbf{bottom-left corner} of the screen. During a meeting, if an emergency occurs (a dog barks, a family member walks in), the user does not need to carefully aim their cursor at the small mic icon. They can \textbf{blindly slam their mouse to the bottom-left corner} and click — the system will instantly force-mute their microphone.

Upon activation, the zone briefly flashes red and a toast confirms: \textbf{``PANIC MUTE activated (Fitts's Law edge target)''}.

This is the most advanced application of Fitts's Law possible in a web application — it exploits the physical constraints of the display hardware itself.

\imageplaceholder{Fitts's Law Panic Mute}{Open index.html, move mouse to the absolute bottom-left corner and click. Screenshot the resulting red flash in the corner and the `PANIC MUTE activated` toast notification.}{Demonstrates Fitts's Law infinite-depth edge targets: the corner zone has zero Index of Difficulty, enabling emergency muting without visual aiming.}
